# Visa Types - Frequently Asked Questions

## Employment-Based Visas

### What is an H-1B visa?
The H-1B visa is a nonimmigrant visa that allows U.S. employers to temporarily employ foreign workers in specialty occupations. Specialty occupations require at least a bachelor's degree or equivalent in a specific field. Common fields include IT, engineering, medicine, accounting, and architecture.

### When is the H-1B lottery?
The H-1B cap registration typically opens in March each year for employment starting October 1. The annual cap is 65,000 visas plus an additional 20,000 for those with U.S. master's degrees or higher. Due to high demand, USCIS uses a lottery system to select petitions.

### What is an L-1 visa?
The L-1 visa allows companies to transfer employees from foreign offices to U.S. offices. L-1A is for managers and executives, while L-1B is for employees with specialized knowledge. The employee must have worked for the company abroad for at least one continuous year within the past three years.

### What is an O-1 visa?
The O-1 visa is for individuals with extraordinary ability in sciences, arts, education, business, athletics, or motion picture/television production. Applicants must demonstrate sustained national or international acclaim and recognition in their field.

## Family-Based Immigration

### Can I sponsor my spouse for a green card?
Yes, U.S. citizens and lawful permanent residents can sponsor their spouses for green cards. Spouses of U.S. citizens are considered "immediate relatives" and are not subject to annual visa caps. Spouses of green card holders fall under a preference category with longer wait times.

### Can I sponsor my parents for immigration?
Only U.S. citizens who are 21 years or older can sponsor their parents for green cards. Parents of U.S. citizens are considered immediate relatives. Green card holders cannot sponsor their parents.

### Can I sponsor my siblings?
U.S. citizens can sponsor siblings for green cards, but this falls under the fourth preference category which has significant backlogs (often 10-20+ years depending on country of origin). Green card holders cannot sponsor siblings.

### What is the affidavit of support?
The affidavit of support (Form I-864) is a legally binding contract where a sponsor agrees to financially support the immigrant. The sponsor must demonstrate income at or above 125% of the federal poverty guidelines for their household size.

## Nonimmigrant Visas

### What is the difference between B-1 and B-2 visas?
B-1 visas are for business visitors (meetings, conferences, negotiations), while B-2 visas are for tourists and those seeking medical treatment. Often these are issued as combined B-1/B-2 visas. Typical validity is up to 6 months per visit.

### What is an F-1 student visa?
The F-1 visa is for academic students attending U.S. colleges, universities, language programs, or other academic institutions. F-1 students can work on-campus and may be eligible for practical training (CPT/OPT) related to their field of study.

### What is ESTA?
ESTA (Electronic System for Travel Authorization) allows citizens of Visa Waiver Program countries to travel to the U.S. for tourism or business for up to 90 days without a visa. ESTA authorization is valid for two years or until your passport expires.

## Green Cards and Permanent Residence

### What is adjustment of status?
Adjustment of status (Form I-485) allows eligible individuals already in the U.S. to apply for permanent residence without leaving the country. Eligibility depends on how you entered the U.S. and your current status.

### What is consular processing?
Consular processing is when an immigrant visa application is processed at a U.S. embassy or consulate abroad. This is required for applicants outside the U.S. or those not eligible for adjustment of status.

### How long is a green card valid?
Permanent resident cards (green cards) are typically valid for 10 years and must be renewed. Conditional green cards (for recent marriages) are valid for 2 years and require removal of conditions. The underlying permanent resident status does not expire with the card.

## Citizenship and Naturalization

### When can I apply for citizenship?
Generally, you can apply for naturalization after 5 years as a permanent resident (3 years if married to a U.S. citizen). You must have been physically present in the U.S. for at least half that time and demonstrate continuous residence.

### What are the requirements for naturalization?
Requirements include: lawful permanent residence for required period, physical presence and continuous residence, good moral character, English language proficiency, knowledge of U.S. civics, and an oath of allegiance.

### Do I need to speak English to become a citizen?
Yes, applicants must demonstrate the ability to read, write, and speak basic English. Exceptions exist for those 50+ with 20 years as a permanent resident, or 55+ with 15 years as a permanent resident.

## DACA

### What is DACA?
DACA (Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals) provides temporary protection from deportation and work authorization for individuals who came to the U.S. as children. DACA does not provide a path to permanent residence or citizenship.

### Can I still apply for DACA?
The DACA program has experienced legal challenges. Currently, only renewals are being accepted. Please contact our office for the most current information on DACA eligibility and applications.

### How often must I renew DACA?
DACA must be renewed every two years. We recommend filing your renewal application 150-120 days before your current DACA expires to ensure continuous work authorization.
